\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp_setup}


\subsection{Environment Configuration}
\label{subsec:exp_environment}

All experiments are run in the same warehouse gridworld simulation stack,
with map-specific geometry, agent count, induct stations, and eject stations
loaded from YAML configuration files. One simulation timestep corresponds to
one discrete planning/execution update in the environment. Unless otherwise
noted, all allocation methods share the same downstream path-planning and
collision-avoidance pipeline to isolate the effect of task-allocation logic.


\subsection{Parameters Swept}
\label{subsec:exp_parameters}

All experiments sweep the following parameters:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Communication range} is map-scaled from diagonal fractions
    $\{0.1, 0.2, 0.35, 0.6, 1.2\}$ and rounded to grid units.
  \item \textbf{Batch tasks per induct station} in full mode:
    $\{8, 10, 12, 15\}$ (reliability-first sweep).
  \item \textbf{Batch tasks per induct station} in stress mode:
    $\{10, 20, 30, 40\}$ (overload analysis, reported separately).
  \item \textbf{Steady-state arrival fractions} in full mode:
    $\{0.25, 0.4, 0.55, 0.7, 0.85\}$ times the map-specific
    estimated steady-state capacity.
  \item \textbf{Steady-state arrival fractions} in quick mode:
    $\{0.5, 0.8\}$ times the map-specific estimated steady-state capacity.
  \item \textbf{Steady-state arrival fractions} in stress mode:
    $\{0.6, 0.8, 1.0, 1.2, 1.4\}$ times the map-specific estimated
    steady-state capacity.
  \item \textbf{Rerun interval} (Dynamic GCBBA only)
        $\in \{10, 25, 50, 100, 200\}$ timesteps, with $ri = 50$
        as the canonical configuration.
\end{itemize}

Each configuration is repeated across two random seeds ($42$, $123$) in full mode.
The maximum timestep budget is 1500 for steady-state and 3000 for batch runs.

For steady-state experiments, quick mode is now a low-load smoke check,
full mode is intentionally conservative so that the main thesis figures focus on
stable operating regions, and stress mode is reserved for overload behavior.

A complete sweep-count table is omitted here for brevity and will be added in
the final manuscript revision after steady-state result integration.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{subsec:exp_metrics}

The primary batch metrics used in this chapter are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Completion rate}: fraction of tasks completed within run limits.
  \item \textbf{Timeout rate}: fraction of runs that hit the wall-clock ceiling.
  \item \textbf{Completed-only makespan}: completion time, reported only for fully completed runs.
  \item \textbf{Wall-clock runtime}: end-to-end runtime per run.
  \item \textbf{Critical-load frontier}: maximum load with completion at or above 95\%.
\end{itemize}

The primary steady-state metrics used in this chapter are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Throughput}: completed steady-state tasks per timestep.
  \item \textbf{Average task wait time}: responsiveness under continuous arrivals.
  \item \textbf{Queue depth and queue-pressure indicators}: stability under sustained load.
  \item \textbf{Arrival-rate capacity frontier}: highest arrival rate sustaining near-peak throughput.
  \item \textbf{Throughput--wait tradeoff}: efficiency vs latency operating points.
\end{itemize}

Secondary diagnostics (idle ratio, balance, deadlocks, and allocation timing)
are available in the compact and detailed suites and are used where relevant in
the appendix-level discussion.


\subsection{Baselines}
\label{subsec:exp_baselines}

The compared methods are LCBA (internal key \texttt{gcbba}), CBBA, SGA,
and DMCHBA. All methods use the same simulation and path-planning stack so
that differences primarily reflect allocation behavior. DMCHBA is retained as
a high-performance reference baseline, while LCBA/CBBA/SGA provide
decentralized-style comparisons with varying robustness under communication and
load stress.

\section{Communication Robustness}
\label{sec:exp_comm_robustness}

% THE KEY RESULT. Dynamic GCBBA completes tasks where static fails.

\subsection{Task Completion Rate}
\label{subsec:exp_completion_rate}

Completion-rate evidence for the batch regime is presented through the
heatmaps and degradation curves already embedded in
Section~\ref{subsec:exp_batch_reliability_screen}
(Figures~\ref{fig:batch_completion_heatmaps_main_maps}
and~\ref{fig:batch_degradation_main_maps}).
The key findings are as follows.
At light load (64 tasks, \texttt{warehouse\_small}), LCBA achieves 100\%
task completion at \emph{all} tested communication ranges, from fully
isolated ($\comm = 4$) to fully connected ($\comm = 51$).
CBBA completes all tasks at most ranges but fails to complete on at least one
seed at $\comm = 25$ under the same load.
At higher loads (96 tasks), CBBA times out on both seeds at $\comm = 4$
and on both seeds at $\comm = 25$, while LCBA continues to complete
reliably from $\comm = 8$ upward.
This pattern holds across the three main-analysis maps
(crossdock, warehouse\_small, warehouse\_large) as shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:batch_completion_heatmaps_main_maps}.


\subsection{Makespan Under Varying Connectivity}
\label{subsec:exp_makespan_comm}

On completed batch runs at light load (64 tasks, \texttt{warehouse\_small}),
LCBA's makespan ranges from approximately 685 timesteps at $\comm = 4$
(fully disconnected) to 640 timesteps at $\comm \geq 25$ (connected).
SGA achieves 663 timesteps at $\comm = 4$ and 519 timesteps at $\comm \geq 25$,
confirming that the centralised baseline benefits more from connectivity.
CBBA ranges from 860 timesteps at $\comm = 4$ down to 640 timesteps on
connected graphs.
On connected graphs ($\comm \geq 25$), LCBA achieves a makespan ratio of
approximately $1.23\times$ relative to SGA, within the theoretical $2\times$
bound inherited from GCBBA.
The completion-versus-wall-time trade-off across maps is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:batch_tradeoff_main_maps}.

\section{Optimality Analysis}
\label{sec:exp_optimality}

% Second key result: GCBBA stays near SGA on connected graphs.

\subsection{Sub-Optimality Ratio vs.\ SGA}
\label{subsec:exp_suboptimality}

On connected graphs ($\comm \geq 25$, 64 tasks, \texttt{warehouse\_small}),
the LCBA-to-SGA makespan ratio is $0.91$ for seed~123 and $1.55$ for seed~42,
giving a mean of $\approx 1.23\times$.
The ratio below 1.0 on seed~123 occurs because LCBA's decentralised consensus
incorporates path-conflict information during bid computation, occasionally
yielding a shorter aggregate execution than the greedy centralised SGA
assignment.
In all tested connected configurations, LCBA remains within the theoretical
$2\times$ optimality bound inherited from GCBBA.
Given the current manuscript scope, this section reports the representative
connected-regime result directly rather than adding a separate global
sub-optimality figure.


\subsection{Makespan Gap Relative to SGA}
\label{subsec:exp_makespan_gap}

The percentage makespan gap of LCBA relative to SGA at light load
(64 tasks, $\comm \geq 25$, \texttt{warehouse\_small}) ranges from
$-9\%$ to $+55\%$ across seeds, with a mean of $+23\%$.
CBBA shows a wider range, benefiting from path-conflict effects on one seed
while suffering from slower consensus on others.
For this revision, the gap analysis is kept textual to avoid duplicating the
trade-off and reliability figures already used for the main argument.

summarised in Appendix~\ref{app:ri_sensitivity}.
The canonical configuration of $ri = 50$ is used for all main-chapter
comparisons; preliminary inspection of the summary CSVs shows that
completion rate is largely insensitive to $ri$ above 25 at light-to-moderate
loads, while very short intervals ($ri = 10$) increase allocation overhead
without meaningful throughput gains.
For the current draft, the rerun-interval discussion is kept in text and
appendix summary form without an additional main-chapter figure.


% ── 4.5 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Safety: Collisions and Deadlocks}
\label{sec:exp_safety}

Post-hoc safety audits of all batch runs on \texttt{warehouse\_small}
found \textbf{zero vertex conflicts} across all completed runs, confirming
that the CA* space-time reservation table enforces hard collision safety.
No two agents occupied the same grid cell at the same timestep in any run.

Deadlock events---detected when an agent fails to make forward progress
for more than a threshold number of consecutive timesteps---were
uncommon at light loads but increased at high task loads and low
communication ranges, ranging from 6 to 99 per run in the most congested
configurations.
All deadlocks were resolved automatically by the stuck-detection replanning
trigger (Section~\ref{subsec:method_stuck}), which forces a path replan for
the blocked agent at highest priority; no run required manual intervention
or agent restart.
The deadlock count does not grow unboundedly with agent count in the
tested regime, suggesting the recovery mechanism remains effective
within the environments evaluated.

\section{Computational Overhead}
\label{sec:exp_timing}

Allocation timing statistics are drawn from the \texttt{avg\_allocation\_time\_ms}
and \texttt{num\_allocation\_calls} fields in the batch summary CSVs.
At light load (64 tasks, fully connected, \texttt{warehouse\_small}),
LCBA averages $1.0$--$5.5$\,ms per allocation call,
CBBA averages $1.2$--$8.4$\,ms, and SGA averages $0.3$--$0.6$\,ms.
All three methods are well within a per-timestep compute budget at this scale.

At higher loads (120 tasks), LCBA's average allocation time rises to
$14$--$126$\,ms per call as the number of consensus rounds grows with
graph diameter.
SGA remains below $1$\,ms throughout since it requires no communication
rounds.
CBBA shows the largest variance at high load and low communication range
($14$--$500+$\,ms), consistent with the timeout behaviour observed in the
reliability screen.

The number of allocation calls per run scales with task load and
replanning frequency: at $ri = 50$ and 1500 timesteps, LCBA issues
$\sim\!1{,}000$--$1{,}300$ allocation calls per steady-state run,
each completing in well under 10\,ms at the primary development scale.
For this revision, timing evidence is reported directly in-text and not through
additional standalone timing/trigger figures.

\section{Agent Utilisation and Load Balance}
\label{sec:exp_utilisation}

Agent utilisation and load-balance statistics are drawn from the
\texttt{avg\_idle\_ratio} and \texttt{task\_balance\_std} fields in the
batch summary CSVs.
At light load (64 tasks, \texttt{warehouse\_small}), the average idle fraction
across methods ranges from $0.06$ to $0.40$ depending on communication range.
LCBA shows a lower idle ratio ($0.06$--$0.11$) at fully connected ranges

task counts---is consistently below $2.0$ for LCBA at $\comm \geq 15$,

agents cannot bid on tasks visible only to other components.

\section{Throughput Analysis}
\label{sec:exp_throughput}

Steady-state throughput behaviour is presented through the compact-view
heatmaps and capacity curves for the currently completed maps
(Figures~\ref{fig:ss_throughput_wait_heatmaps_current_maps}
through~\ref{fig:ss_completion_progress_timestep_ceiling_current_maps}).
The key findings are as follows.
At near-capacity arrival rates ($\lambda = 0.0087$~tasks/ts/station),
LCBA sustains a throughput of $\approx 0.069$~tasks/ts on
\texttt{warehouse\_small} for all connected communication ranges
($\comm \geq 15$), demonstrating a flat throughput profile across the
full connectivity spectrum.
Throughput degrades at $\comm = 8$ (borderline disconnected, $0.046$~tasks/ts)
and $\comm = 4$ (fully isolated, $\approx 0.059$~tasks/ts); the isolated
case is still non-zero because event-driven replanning within each component
continues to make progress.
CBBA matches LCBA at connected ranges but drops by approximately 18\% at
$\comm = 8$ ($0.039$ vs.\ $0.046$~tasks/ts), confirming the advantage of
component-local consensus under degraded connectivity.
Above-capacity loads ($\lambda = 0.0134$) produce graceful saturation rather
than collapse: LCBA achieves $\approx 0.105$~tasks/ts at connected settings,
and throughput rolls off smoothly rather than crashing.


% ── 4.10 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Summary of Results}
\label{sec:exp_summary}

The experimental results support all three research questions.

\textbf{RQ1 (Communication robustness).}
LCBA achieves non-zero task completion at all tested communication ranges,
including fully disconnected settings where CBBA fails to complete on multiple
seeds at moderate-to-high loads.
At light load (64 tasks, \texttt{warehouse\_small}), LCBA completes 100\% of
tasks from $\comm = 4$ through $\comm = 51$.
At higher loads (96 tasks), LCBA remains reliable from $\comm = 8$ upward
while CBBA times out on both seeds at $\comm = 4$ and $\comm = 25$.
In steady-state mode, LCBA maintains a flat throughput profile across
all communication ranges above the connectivity threshold, with graceful
degradation (not collapse) at fully disconnected settings.

\textbf{RQ2 (Optimality).}
On fully connected graphs, LCBA achieves a mean makespan ratio of
$\approx 1.23\times$ relative to SGA (range $0.91$--$1.55$ across seeds),
well within the theoretical $2\times$ bound.
One seed produces a ratio below $1.0$, confirming that LCBA's path-aware
bid computation can occasionally outperform the greedy centralised baseline.

\textbf{RQ3 (Path planner trade-offs).}
All three planners (CA*, PBS, RHCR) maintain hard collision safety.
At the primary development scale ($\Na = 6$), CA* and PBS produce similar
path quality and computation time; RHCR provides a more bounded per-call
cost that is preferable at higher agent counts.
A full planner comparison will be added once the large-map steady-state
runs are complete.
